# Discussion

## 5.1 Overview

This study examined engagement profiles and learning trajectories among students interacting with online continuous assessments in a higher education LMS environment, and assessed how these patterns relate to final examination outcomes. Four research questions were addressed through a combination of composite scoring, linear trend analysis, and non-parametric group comparisons. The findings reveal a cohort characterised by strong overall engagement, but with meaningful sub-groups exhibiting distinct behavioural patterns and divergent summative outcomes. Three themes emerging from the data warrant extended discussion: the polarised nature of student engagement, the counterintuitive performance advantage of Decliners, and the partial — rather than full — equivalence between Improvers and Steady High Performers.

---

## 5.2 Engagement Polarisation Rather Than Heterogeneous Distribution (RQ1)

The engagement profile distribution revealed a strikingly polarised cohort rather than the broadly heterogeneous spread anticipated. The overwhelming majority of students (83.5%) were classified as High engagement, while Medium engagement students were notably scarce (4.8%), and Low engagement students comprised 11.7% of all enrollments. This bimodal pattern — with a dominant High cluster and a secondary Low cluster separated by a sparse middle band — diverges from prior studies that typically report more evenly distributed engagement tiers (Broadbent & Poon, 2015; Gašević et al., 2016).

This polarisation may reflect structural features of the institutional context. In the study setting, continuous assessment quizzes are embedded within a graded LMS environment where quiz performance contributes to the overall course grade. Such high-stakes integration is known to reduce voluntary disengagement among students who are otherwise motivated to perform academically (Pekrun et al., 2014). Students who engage at all tend to engage fully, while those who disengage do so almost entirely — consistent with the near-zero participation rates observed in the Low engagement group (mean participation rate = 2.72%).

The 9.8% of students who recorded zero quiz attempts represents a distinct and educationally concerning sub-group. These students are not merely low performers — they are non-participants whose disengagement from formative processes is complete and therefore not captured by any quiz-based metric. This finding aligns with research on "ghost students" in LMS environments (Coates, 2007), who remain enrolled but generate no digital learning traces. From an intervention design perspective, this group requires targeted outreach strategies that are fundamentally different from strategies suited to low-performing but participatory students.

The small size of the Medium engagement group (4.8%) suggests that partial engagement — attempting some quizzes but not all — is an unstable behavioural state in this context. Students appear to quickly resolve toward either full participation or withdrawal, leaving little sustained middle ground. This has practical implications for early warning systems: a student transitioning from High to Medium engagement may be signalling an imminent shift toward full disengagement rather than a minor fluctuation.

## 5.3 The Decliner Paradox: High Summative Outcomes Despite Declining Formative Engagement (RQ2, RQ4)

The most unexpected finding of this study was the superior final examination performance of Decliners (mean ESE = 76.33%) relative to all other trajectory groups, including Steady High Performers (73.91%). This counterintuitive pattern — that students whose quiz scores decline over the semester outperform those who maintain consistently high performance — challenges the prevailing assumption in learning analytics that sustained formative engagement is a prerequisite for strong summative outcomes (Tempelaar et al., 2015; Lust et al., 2013).

Several explanatory mechanisms warrant consideration. First, Decliners as a group were characterised by relatively high average quiz scores (68.40%) alongside strongly negative slopes (mean slope = −10.41), indicating that their declining trajectory begins from a position of considerable prior knowledge. These students may have entered the course with robust foundational competencies, performed well on early quizzes by drawing on existing knowledge, and subsequently reduced their quiz engagement as the semester progressed — perhaps redirecting attention toward examination preparation, other courses, or more advanced self-directed study. Their high ESE scores may thus reflect genuine subject mastery that was established early and sustained independently of continued quiz participation.

Second, declining quiz performance does not necessarily signal declining learning. In LMS environments where quiz questions are repeated or predictable, high early scores may reflect both knowledge and familiarity with the quiz format, while later score reductions may reflect task fatigue or strategic effort allocation rather than knowledge decay (Kizilcec et al., 2017). The ESE, as a distinct high-stakes instrument, may be a more robust measure of true subject mastery, and Decliners' strong performance on it may reveal a decoupling between formative quiz engagement and summative examination capability.

Third, the Decliner trajectory may capture a heterogeneous group that includes students who were genuinely disengaging from formative tasks while sustaining or intensifying examination preparation activities not visible in the LMS data. This interpretation is consistent with research showing that high-achieving students in structured curricula frequently adopt strategic approaches to assessment that minimise effort on lower-stakes tasks in favour of preparation for higher-stakes examinations (Entwistle & Ramsden, 1983; Biggs, 1987).

These findings suggest that the uncritical use of declining LMS engagement as an at-risk indicator may generate false positives — flagging students as academically vulnerable when they are in fact well-prepared for their examinations. Intervention systems built on engagement trajectory data should incorporate ESE performance monitoring alongside LMS signals to avoid misclassification of this group.

## 5.4 Multiple Pathways to Academic Success: A Partial Confirmation (RQ4)

A central proposition of this study was that students follow multiple pathways to academic success, with Improvers achieving examination outcomes comparable to Steady High Performers. The data partially supports this proposition. Improvers demonstrated a mean ESE score of 68.06% — meaningfully above Consistently Struggling students (65.02%) and Stable Average students (67.28%) — indicating that within-semester performance improvement does confer some summative advantage. However, the gap between Improvers and Steady High Performers was statistically significant (5.85 percentage points; Mann-Whitney U = 9,652,709, p < 0.001), indicating that improvement during the semester does not fully compensate for the advantage conferred by consistent high performance throughout.

This finding has both theoretical and practical implications. Theoretically, it nuances the learning analytics narrative around "recovery trajectories" (Jayaprakash et al., 2014; Arnold & Pistilli, 2012): while improvement is positively associated with outcomes, the ceiling on that advantage is meaningful. Students who begin the semester with strong quiz performance and maintain it appear to benefit from compound effects of early knowledge consolidation and sustained practice — advantages that late-semester improvement cannot fully replicate within a single semester.

Practically, this suggests that early intervention — targeted at students showing early signs of low engagement or poor quiz performance — is more likely to be impactful than remedial support initiated mid-semester. An Improver trajectory, while preferable to a Decliner or Struggling trajectory, represents a less efficient path to high examination performance than a Steady High Performer trajectory. Adaptive learning systems and educators alike should therefore prioritise proactive engagement strategies at the outset of a course rather than reactive interventions triggered by observed decline.

The non-significant difference between Stable Average and Consistently Struggling students (p = 1.000) is also noteworthy. It suggests that moderate, stable quiz engagement at a mediocre level does not yield meaningfully better outcomes than persistent low performance. This finding challenges the assumption that any level of consistent engagement is beneficial — engagement that remains chronically below a threshold of academic quality appears to offer limited summative value. This underscores the importance of not only monitoring participation but also the quality of formative performance when designing adaptive interventions.

## 5.5 Engagement Profiles as Predictors of ESE Outcomes (RQ3)

The statistically significant association between engagement profiles and ESE outcomes (H(2) = 331.79, p < 0.001) confirms that composite engagement — combining quiz participation and performance — is meaningfully related to final examination scores. All three pairwise comparisons were significant after Bonferroni correction, with a monotonically increasing relationship between engagement level and mean ESE score (Low = 66.19%, Medium = 70.22%, High = 73.18%).

However, the small effect size (ε² = 0.018) warrants careful interpretation. While the relationship is statistically reliable, engagement profile as operationalised in this study accounts for only a modest proportion of the variance in ESE outcomes. This is consistent with findings from the companion Paper 1 analysis, which identified quiz-based features as moderate rather than strong predictors of summative performance, particularly in courses exhibiting ceiling effects on quiz scores. The gap between the Low and High engagement groups (7.0 percentage points in mean ESE) is educationally meaningful but falls short of the magnitude that would justify engagement profile alone as a screening criterion for academic risk identification.

This finding reinforces the argument that formative engagement data should be used as one signal among several in predictive analytics frameworks rather than as a standalone indicator. Composite approaches that integrate engagement profiles, learning trajectories, demographic characteristics, and programme-level factors are likely to yield substantially stronger predictive models.

Collectively, the findings of this study support a differentiated model of student support in which intervention strategies are tailored to engagement profile and trajectory type rather than applied uniformly. Table 7 summarises the recommended intervention implications for each identified group.

This differentiated framework aligns with the principles of adaptive learning environments (Sottilare et al., 2018) and the broader dissertation research agenda of developing hybrid rule-based and reinforcement learning systems (RBS-PPO) for academic performance enhancement. The engagement profiles and trajectory labels generated in this study can serve directly as state variables in the RBS-PPO framework: rule-based conditions can flag at-risk states (Low engagement, Consistent Struggle, early Decliner signals), while the reinforcement learning component can learn optimal intervention sequences from historical outcome data. This conceptual bridge between descriptive classification and adaptive intervention design represents the primary contribution of this paper to the broader research programme.

## 5.7 Limitations

Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, the analysis is based on a single semester of data from one institution, which restricts the generalisability of the findings. The observed polarisation of engagement profiles, in particular, may reflect institution-specific assessment structures rather than universal patterns in LMS-based formative assessment.

Second, the composite engagement score, while theoretically grounded, treats participation and quiz performance as distinct dimensions with fixed weights (60/40). Alternative weighting schemes or multidimensional engagement operationalisations incorporating time-on-task, access frequency, or discussion forum activity may yield different profile distributions and require evaluation in future work.

Third, the trajectory analysis relies on the linear slope of quiz scores as the sole indicator of learning progression. This assumption of linearity may not adequately capture non-linear learning patterns — for instance, students who improve sharply at the mid-semester point and then plateau would not be distinguished from students with a consistently modest positive trend.

Fourth, the data available does not include information on external factors — attendance, part-time employment, personal circumstances — that may independently influence both engagement patterns and examination outcomes. The small effect sizes observed in both RQ3 and RQ4 suggest that substantial unexplained variance remains, likely attributable in part to such unmeasured variables.

Finally, the Decliner paradox identified in this study demands cautious interpretation. The current analysis cannot definitively establish whether Decliners are a strategically disengaging high-ability group, a group whose declining quiz scores reflect genuine disengagement offset by prior knowledge, or a heterogeneous mix of both. Qualitative follow-up — including student interviews or think-aloud protocols — would substantially enrich the interpretation of this trajectory type.

---

## 5.8 Conclusion

This study demonstrates that students exhibit meaningfully diverse engagement profiles and learning trajectories in online continuous assessment environments, and that these patterns are significantly associated with final examination outcomes. The findings challenge two prevailing assumptions: that declining formative engagement predicts poor summative performance, and that within-semester improvement leads to outcomes equivalent to sustained high performance. The polarised engagement distribution, the Decliner paradox, and the partial equivalence between Improvers and Steady High Performers collectively argue for trajectory-sensitive, differentiated intervention strategies over uniform engagement monitoring approaches. These findings contribute empirical grounding to the design of adaptive learning systems and reinforce the case for pattern-specific personalisation in educational support frameworks.
